Introduction

AIM OF PAPER

To identify causal effect of CBI schemes on population mortality and household level heath outcomes.

BURKINA -FASO

  • Land-locked, low-income country in Western Africa;

  • Lack of national health insurance schemes gives rise to micro-insurance schemes;

  • Focus on the Nouna District CBI Program.

NOUNA DISTRICT

  • Low income and literacy levels i.e. representative sample;

  • Comprehensive data collection system has been set-up;

  • 34 Primary care facilities and 1 Secondary.

COMMUNITY-BASED INSURANC

  • Operated by any organization other than the government;

  • Schemes are flexible and easy to set-up;

Objectives:

  1. Protects against financial shocks;

  2. To make healthcare more affordable;

EXISTING LITERATURE

  • RAND experiment: Negative effects of health insurance on health outcomes;

  • PROGRESA program: No impact of insurance on health;

  • Ekman(2004): Insurance increses health service utilization.

METHODOLOGY

  • Data from Health and Demographic Surveillance System (HDSS) (six rounds, 2003-08);

  • Used stepped wedge cluster randomization;

  • Three phases.

  • 48 districts divided into 33 clusters randomly assigned to be targeted in 2004,05,06.

INSURANCE ROLL-OUT

  • Insurance was optional;

  • Comprehensive cover and access to 1 specific public primary location

  • Providers paid on capitation basis (fixed rate)

Yearly insurance premiume:

  1. Children US \$ 0.96 (500 francs);

  2. Adults US \$2.88 (1500 francs);

IMPACT OF CBI: HOUSEHOLD LEVEL

  • High retention rates (85% for households, 59% for individuals);

  • One in six individuals participated in the HDSS survey

INSURANCE ENROLLMENT

  • On average 12.6% enrolled at time of interview;

    • MONETARY IMPACT

    • 6-15% of households experience catastrophic health expenditure;

    • 6% households had a health problem in the 30 days preceding their survey

    • Average cost \$1.2 (self treated) and \$15.6 (professionally)

    • Annual expected out-of-pocket expenditure was approximately same as insurance premium for adults

    Quantifying the impact of insurance on individual out-of-pocket health expenditure:

    $$ Y_{ivt}=\alpha + \beta CBI_{vt} + \gamma X_i+ \delta_t+ \delta_v+\epsilon_{it}$$

  • where: $Y_{ivt}$ : Health expenditure of individual i in village v and year t ;

  • $CBI_{vt}$ Binary village-level indicator

  • $ X_i$ Covariates (age, sex, etc)

  • $\delta_t$, $\delta_v$ Year and village fixed effects

    • Expenditure impact at the individual level

  • Treated appear to spend less than individuals un-treated.;

  • Treated individuals less likely to get treatment and also pay marginally more;

  • Coeff of -0.315 implies the average person saves about \$3.8 per year. (Overestimated);

    • Impact on catastrophic health expenditures

  • 30% reduction in the likelihood of catastrophic expenditures;

    • IMPACT OF CBI: POPULATION LEVEL

  • 14 % increase in total population b/w 2003-08;

  • Mortaility rate;

    • Estimating the population level health impact:

    $$ m_{ivt}=\alpha + \beta CBI_{vt} + \delta_i+ \delta_t+ \delta_v+\epsilon_{ivt}$$

  • where: $m_{ivt}$ : Crude mortality rate of age group i in village v and year t;

  • $CBI_{vt}$ Binary village-level indicator

  • $ X_i$ Covariates (age, sex, etc)

  • $\delta_t$, $\delta_v$, $\delta_i$ Age, Group and Year fixed effects

    • With village-specific time trends;

    TRENDS BY TREATMENT GROUP

  • Aprroximately 40% improvement in child mortality;

  • 65+ group, increase in 2004-05 and drop in 2006;

    • CBI ON PERCEPTIONS OF HEALTH CARE

  • Highest level of dissatisfaction observed in 2005-06;

    • Two test to assess the effect:

    1. Average response on timing of rollout;

    2. Using rollout as IV;

    CONCLUSIONS

    Impact on health

    • Some positive effects on health expenditure;

    • No significant health improvements for children;

    • Negative effect on the health of old people;

    CONCLUSIONS

    Low enrollment in the insurance scheme (below 10%)

    • High premiums;

    • Lack of information;

    • Incentives to health facilities;

    CONCLUSIONS

    Issues with Insurance scheme

    • Not all issues covered;

    • Miscommunication;

    • Unexpected costs would disincentive enrollees;

    • Per capita payments to health facilities;

    CONCLUSIONS

    • Reducing overall premium payments per household;

    • Health workers now paid in two installments;

    • Capitation levels went up for children;

    • Consultation fees are reimbursed;

    • Bonus structure allowed;

    • Well-written paper;

    • Points out its own limitations;
